Billing Policy and Manual Review Guide

Overview

This guide explains how a support assistant should handle subscription billing, invoices, duplicate charge reports, refund requests, and payment disputes. The guidance follows common SaaS billing workflows: explain the process, collect safe identifiers, and route sensitive financial decisions to a human reviewer.

Key Concepts

- Subscription billing commonly creates invoices for recurring service periods, usage, taxes, credits, and adjustments.
- Invoice and payment questions can involve invoice status, subscription renewal, failed payment attempts, duplicate charges, refunds, or account billing details.
- Duplicate charge reports require a human reviewer to compare invoice records and payment processor activity.
- Refund requests require escalation because they involve financial approval.
- The assistant must not promise credits, refunds, compensation, reimbursement, or bank processing timelines.
- Legal threats, formal dispute language, and compliance concerns require human review.

Common Questions

What should happen when a user reports duplicate charges?

Collect safe context such as invoice number, charge date, billing email, account identifier, subscription ID, and a short description of the duplicate charge. Never request full card numbers, bank credentials, or security codes.

Can the assistant approve a refund?

No. The assistant can acknowledge the request, explain that a human billing reviewer will check invoice and payment records, and prepare the handoff JSON.

Why can billing review take time?

A human reviewer may need to compare invoice records, payment attempts, refunds, account state, subscription status, and payment processor records before making a decision.

Troubleshooting

1. Identify whether the issue is invoice amount, duplicate charge, refund, payment failure, renewal timing, or tax/billing details.
2. Ask for invoice number, charge date, billing email, account identifier, or subscription ID when available.
3. Do not collect full payment card numbers, bank credentials, or security codes.
4. Escalate billing disputes, refund requests, duplicate charge reports, and payment corrections.
5. Escalate legal or compliance concerns without giving legal advice.

Best Practices

- Keep billing explanations process-focused.
- Avoid promising a financial outcome.
- Preserve all escalation reasons.
- Include safe billing identifiers and retrieved sources in the handoff JSON.
